Background

I trained as a chemist, but I discovered early on that the most interesting problems in chemistry are not solved at the bench — they are solved by understanding patterns. Water treatment is full of these patterns: how a molecule's structure determines whether it survives a treatment process, how dozens of water quality parameters interact to produce a harmful byproduct, how small decisions in plant design have large downstream consequences.

Programming gave me a way to formalize those patterns. My first models were simple regressions. Over time I moved into more sophisticated approaches — cheminformatics, structure–activity relationships, data pipelines — but the underlying motivation stayed the same: build something useful that could not exist without computation.

My career has spanned two major domains. First, drinking water treatment, where I built models to predict the formation of disinfection byproducts — chlorinated and brominated compounds that form when disinfectants react with natural organic matter. Second, wastewater treatment, where I modeled the removal efficiency of organic micropollutants based on their molecular structure and the conditions of the treatment process.

What drives me

Water is not an abstract problem. Billions of people depend on treatment systems that are designed, operated, and optimized by engineers working with incomplete information. A model that helps an engineer make a better decision — without running a three-month experiment — has real consequences for real people.

That tangibility is what keeps me motivated. I am not interested in building models for their own sake. I want to understand the problem deeply enough that the model actually reflects something true about the system, and I want the output to be useful to someone who does not share my technical background.

I am also drawn to the combination of domains. Environmental chemistry, informatics, data science, and software engineering each have their own communities and their own blind spots. I find myself most useful — and most engaged — at the edges, where those communities meet and where the existing tools are not quite the right fit.

How I work

I tend to start with the simplest model that could possibly be useful, then add complexity only when the data demands it. This has saved me from many elegant solutions to the wrong problem. When I am uncertain about the right approach, I would rather build two simple things and compare them than commit to one complicated thing and rationalize it afterward.

I think documentation and reproducibility are part of the work, not afterthoughts. A model that cannot be understood by a collaborator or reconstructed from scratch is a liability, not an asset. I write code as if someone else will have to read it — which is usually true, and that someone is often me six months later.

I am comfortable working independently on long-horizon problems, but I value direct feedback and collaboration early in a project. The best projects I have worked on involved frequent short conversations with domain experts who pushed back on my assumptions.

What I'm looking for

I am looking for work where the technical problems are connected to meaningful outcomes — environmental, public health, scientific, or otherwise. I am most effective when I can bring both chemical domain knowledge and programming skills to bear on the same problem, rather than serving in a purely technical or purely scientific role.

I thrive in environments that value rigor without bureaucracy: where a wrong answer that was honestly pursued is treated as useful information, and where good work can speak for itself regardless of job title.

Practically: I am open to research, industry, and applied science roles. Remote-friendly environments are a strong preference. I am happy to work across time zones.
