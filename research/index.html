Drinking water treatment and DBPs

Drinking water treatment relies on disinfection — typically with chlorine, chloramines, or ozone — to inactivate pathogens before water reaches consumers. The problem is that disinfectants do not react selectively. When they encounter the natural organic matter present in source water (decomposed plant and animal material), they produce a range of unintended compounds called disinfection byproducts, or DBPs.

Many DBPs are regulated because of their association with adverse health outcomes at chronic exposure levels. The most studied are trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs), but hundreds of others have been identified, most of them unstudied. Treatment plants must balance effective disinfection against minimizing byproduct formation — a tradeoff that depends on source water chemistry, treatment design, and seasonal variation.

My work in this area focused on building predictive models for DBP formation. Rather than prescribing a single treatment solution, these models give operators and engineers a way to anticipate how changes in water chemistry or disinfectant dose will affect byproduct yields — before making changes to an operating system.

Wastewater and micropollutants

Wastewater treatment plants are designed primarily to remove bulk organic matter, nutrients, and pathogens. They were not designed for the growing list of organic micropollutants — pharmaceuticals, personal care products, industrial chemicals, pesticides — that enter the water cycle through household and industrial discharge.

These compounds are present at very low concentrations (nanograms to micrograms per liter), but many are biologically active at those concentrations. Some are endocrine disruptors. Some are persistent. Conventional wastewater treatment removes some of them well and others poorly, but predicting which compounds will be removed under which conditions requires understanding both the chemistry of the compound and the characteristics of the treatment process.

My models in this area use molecular descriptors — computed properties derived from chemical structure — to predict removal efficiency without requiring compound-specific experimental data. This is useful for regulators and treatment designers evaluating compounds that have not yet been tested, or for assessing the relative risk of treatment configurations.

Modeling approach

Both research areas share a common challenge: the systems are complex, data are expensive to generate, and the people who need the models are not statisticians. This shapes how I build models.

I favor interpretable models over black boxes when the audience includes non-computational practitioners. A regression with meaningful coefficients — where a chemist can look at the model and say "yes, that makes chemical sense" — is more useful than a deep learning model with marginally better cross-validation error and no interpretability. The goal is a model that earns trust from the people who will use it, not just one that performs well on held-out data.

That said, I do not treat interpretability as an absolute constraint. For some problems, the data structure or the prediction task genuinely requires more expressive models. The question I ask first is always: what decision does this model need to support, and what does the person making that decision need to understand about the output?

Tools and methods

Most of my computational work is done in Python. For cheminformatics tasks, I work with RDKit — generating molecular descriptors, fingerprints, and structure-based features from SMILES representations. For modeling, I use scikit-learn for conventional machine learning and statistical approaches.

Data wrangling is done with pandas; visualization with matplotlib and seaborn. For structure–activity relationship (QSAR) work specifically, I follow OECD validation principles: defined endpoint, unambiguous algorithm, defined applicability domain, appropriate goodness-of-fit and robustness statistics, and a mechanistic interpretation where possible.
